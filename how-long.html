How Long Does It Take to Get a California Real Estate License?

For most people: 3 to 6 months — and unlike other states, part of that is legally mandatory. The DRE's pacing rules mean the coursework alone can't be finished in under about 54 days, no matter how motivated you are. Here's the whole timeline.

The Short Answer

California Has a Legal Speed Limit

Most states let you binge the coursework as fast as you can absorb it. California doesn't. DRE rules require that each of the three required courses take a minimum of 2½ weeks (18 days) — so the 135 hours of education has a hard floor of roughly 54 days, even for someone studying around the clock. That's why every honest answer to "how fast can I get licensed in California" starts at about three months, and why anyone promising two weeks is selling something.

The realistic picture: motivated full-time people land around 3–4 months; the nights-and-weekends majority takes 4–6. Here's the pipeline, and — as with everything bureaucratic — the trick is overlapping the steps:

1
The Three Courses (135 Hours)
~8 weeks minimum

Real Estate Principles, Real Estate Practice, and one elective — each with the 18-day DRE minimum. Fully online is standard. Even with the pacing floor, this is where your effort matters: finishing each course the day its 18-day window opens keeps you at the ~8-week minimum instead of drifting to four months.

2
Apply — Ideally the Combo Application
Can overlap step 1

The combined exam + license application ($450) is the timeline move: one filing covers both, so passing the exam flows straight into licensure instead of starting a second application queue. You'll also complete fingerprinting (Live Scan in California; out-of-state applicants use the FD-258 card route).

3
DRE Review → Exam Scheduling
A few weeks, varies

DRE processes your application, then you self-schedule your exam through eLicensing. Processing speed is the most variable part of the California timeline — see the snapshot below for where it currently stands. Complete, deficiency-free applications move much faster; anything DRE has to query adds weeks.

4
Take the Exam
Scheduling-dependent

150 questions, 3 hours, 70% to pass. Seat availability at DRE exam centers ebbs and flows; booking promptly when your approval lands is worth real calendar time. If it goes wrong, the retake process is straightforward and your application stays valid for two years.

5
License Issued
Fast with the combo

With the combo application, passing rolls directly into license issuance once your fingerprints and documents are on file. The license itself doesn't require a hiring broker to be issued — but you'll need one before you can actually work, so line that up while you study and this step costs nothing.

📸

Snapshot — July 2026

DRE notes its processing timeframes are staffing-impacted and updates them weekly; recent clean applications have been moving in roughly 2–3 weeks, with deficient ones taking substantially longer. For today's actual numbers, check DRE's current processing timeframes page — it's the live source this paragraph will eventually be staler than.

Where Timelines Die

The Avoidable Delays

The California-specific failure modes: filing exam-only instead of the combo (congratulations, you've queued twice), application deficiencies (the single biggest source of multi-week detours — DRE has no expedite lane to rescue you), slow exam booking when your approval arrives, and failing the 150 — which roughly half of first-timers do, and which restarts your scheduling clock plus a $100 retake fee.

💡

The 18-day rule has a silver lining: built-in prep time. You literally cannot rush the courses — so use the mandated weeks to drill practice questions alongside the coursework. Californians who treat the pacing floor as exam prep time walk in over-prepared without spending a single extra day.
