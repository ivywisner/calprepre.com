California Real Estate Reciprocity: There's None — Here's the Path

The DRE waives nothing for out-of-state agents — not the courses, not the 150-question exam. But you don't have to live in California to get licensed, and more of your education may transfer than you think.

The Short Answer

Does California Have Real Estate Reciprocity?

The DRE doesn't leave room for interpretation:

"California has no reciprocity with any other state to allow a waiver of any of the requirements to obtain a license."
— California Department of Real Estate

That's stricter than most states. Texas will at least waive the national exam portion for licensed out-of-state agents; Florida has mutual recognition with ten states. California waives nothing — a twenty-year Nevada broker and a brand-new applicant face the same requirements: the education, the application, the fingerprints, and the same 150-question exam with the same 70% pass bar.

The Silver Linings

What Does Carry Over

No reciprocity doesn't mean starting from absolute zero. Two things genuinely work in an out-of-state applicant's favor:

✓ What out-of-state applicants keep
  • ✓ No residency requirement. You can hold a California license while living in Phoenix, Dallas, or anywhere else — no move required, before or after licensing.
  • ✓ Education can transfer. The DRE accepts transcripts from accredited out-of-state institutions toward the three required courses (three semester-units or four quarter-units each). If your real estate education came from an accredited school, submit the transcripts before paying for courses you may not need.
What never transfers: your license itself, your exam results from another state, and your years of experience — none of it shortens the California path.
The Mechanics

How Out-of-State Applicants Get Licensed

  1. Satisfy the education requirement — the three college-level courses, via California providers or accredited out-of-state transcripts the DRE accepts.
  2. Apply for the exam and, as a nonresident, file the Consent to Service of Process (Form RE 234) — completed, signed, and notarized. It designates California as able to accept legal service on your behalf; it's a form, not a hurdle.
  3. Complete fingerprinting acceptable to the California DOJ — out-of-state applicants use the FBI FD-258 card route through local law enforcement.
  4. Pass the 150-question exam — 70% to pass, same as every California applicant.
⚠️

Ignore any site promising a California "license transfer." There's no such mechanism — the DRE's no-waiver language is absolute. Anyone selling a shortcut is selling the standard path with extra steps and a markup.

💡

Study advice for experienced agents: the national principles will feel like review — your exposure is California-specific law and practice. Agency disclosure rules, community property, the Subdivided Lands Law, transfer disclosure statements — that's the material that decides whether experienced out-of-state agents pass, and it's exactly what your home state never covered.
